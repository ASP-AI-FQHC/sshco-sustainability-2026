Section One

Our Purpose as a Committee & the Urgency Before Us

The three of us — Selena King, Brian Alexander, and Guy Fuller — have seen enough of this organization's work to know exactly what would be lost if SSHCO's funding were to expire without a clear path forward. We are not here to manage a sunset. We are here to prevent one. The sustainability of SSHCO is not a financial abstraction — it is a direct expression of our commitment to the South Side residents who have come to rely on this organization for the care, navigation, and support that no other single entity in their lives provides. With less than twelve months of current funding remaining, this committee is prepared to move with the clarity and urgency that this moment requires.

The Funding Cliff: SSHCO's current state transformation funding expires within one year. This committee's work must close meaningful commitments across four coordinated funding tracks before that date. Waiting for a single large grant or a single anchor partner is not a strategy — it is a risk. Our approach must be diversified, parallel, disciplined, and execution-focused.

The foundation of everything we do is a compelling, data-driven, and emotionally resonant case for support. Before we approach a single foundation, corporation, health system, or managed care partner, we must ensure that our materials are sharp, our impact narrative is tight, and our numbers are current. The story we have to tell is genuinely powerful: a collaborative of 13 safety-net healthcare providers serving 15 zip codes, connecting thousands of Medicaid-enrolled and uninsured residents not just to doctors but to food, housing, transportation, and the social scaffolding that accounts for up to 70 percent of a person's health outcomes. We are not asking for charity. We are offering proven infrastructure in exchange for partnership.

The committee's work is organized around four parallel funding tracks, each requiring a distinct relationship strategy and a realistic timeline to commitment. Our goal is not to find one savior funder. It is to build a diversified revenue base that reduces SSHCO's vulnerability to the kind of funding disruption we are currently facing, while using AI selectively to improve speed, discipline, and consistency. What follows is our map for doing exactly that.

Section Two

Our Four-Track Strategy

The committee will work simultaneously across four distinct funding tracks. Each track has its own relationship logic, timeline, and revenue potential. None of them should wait for another to mature before we begin. The committee should assign primary ownership for each track among its three members, with all members supporting where their networks and relationships create openings.

Track One
Institutional Philanthropy & Foundations
Targeted grant applications to local and national foundations with active cycles directly aligned to our model. Several have imminent deadlines we cannot afford to miss. This is our most immediate source of revenue and requires us to move on applications now.
Immediate Action Required
Track Two
Corporate Sponsorship & ESG Investment
Structured tiered giving programs for corporations with documented South Side health equity commitments. This track carries our highest single-gift potential. Relationships need to open now so they can close within our window. AbbVie and Advocate Health Care are our anchor targets.
Highest Gift Potential
Track Three
Health System Community Benefit
Every nonprofit hospital in our collaborative has IRS-mandated community benefit obligations. We need to formalize SSHCO as a documented line item in each partner's community benefit portfolio. UChicago Medicine invested $715M in community benefits in FY2024 — we need to be formally part of that story.
Internal Priority
Track Four
Medicaid Managed Care Organization Investment
Illinois MCOs bear direct financial risk for the same Medicaid population we serve. When SSHCO coordinates care — connecting patients to primary care, providing rides, stabilizing social needs — we directly reduce costly emergency department visits and avoidable hospitalizations. This is not a philanthropic case to make; it is an actuarial one. The committee should commission a brief economic impact analysis that quantifies the avoided costs SSHCO generates for managed care plans operating in our 15 zip codes, and use that as the entry point for value-based investment conversations.

Section Four

Corporate Partnership & Sponsorship Strategy

The corporate track is where our largest individual commitments will come from, but it requires a fundamentally different relationship logic than grant-seeking. Corporations give when they see alignment between our mission and their own business interests, brand values, employee engagement priorities, and ESG reporting needs. Our job is to make that alignment unmistakably clear — and to customize the conversation for each prospect rather than using a one-size-fits-all pitch.

AbbVie is our single most important corporate prospect. They have already demonstrated extraordinary commitment to South Side health equity — $75 million to UChicago Medicine's cancer pavilion and a separate $8 million specifically to reduce healthcare disparities on the South Side. Since UChicago Medicine is our anchor partner, we have a direct and credible path to AbbVie's corporate philanthropy leadership through that relationship. The committee should request a senior-level introduction from UChicago Medicine's community benefit team, and the ask should be structured as a multi-year naming or presenting sponsorship — not a small grant. We should be thinking at a level commensurate with AbbVie's documented commitment in this space.

Advocate Health Care has announced a $1 billion investment to expand access to primary care, specialty care, and wellness services on Chicago's South Side — a commitment aligned with the continuing life expectancy gap documented in current CDPH data. The 2023 CDPH brief shows roughly a 17- to 18-year gap between North Side and South Side community areas, with an even larger 20.7-year gap between the city's highest- and lowest-life-expectancy communities. That is our mission. In the same geography. With the same population. The committee needs to initiate a strategic conversation with Advocate's community health and philanthropic leadership. The framing should be partnership, not grant: SSHCO's navigation infrastructure is a force multiplier for Advocate's capital investments, and the conversation should be held at a level where that logic lands.

The Pritzker Traubert Foundation recently awarded $5 million to City Colleges of Chicago and Cook County Health for healthcare workforce development. SSHCO's community health workers are precisely the kind of frontline workforce the foundation invests in. The committee should explore board-level connections to Penny Pritzker's team and approach with a workforce development framing — positioning our CHW training and deployment model as the community workforce infrastructure piece that complements the institutional healthcare investments the foundation is already making.

Section Five

AI-Enabled Sustainability Infrastructure

AI should be treated as practical sustainability infrastructure for SSHCO — not as a separate initiative, and not as a substitute for relationships, judgment, or community trust. Used well, it can help a small committee move faster, operate more consistently, and produce stronger external materials across foundation outreach, corporate partnership development, board recruitment, health-system alignment, and Medicaid managed care conversations.

In SSHCO's case, the value of AI is straightforward: it can help translate real operating outcomes into repeatable strategy assets. SSHCO already has a strong factual base — 80,000+ patient visits, 7,000+ social service connections, 2,800+ rides, 60+ providers, and 21 care sites — but those results need to be turned into sharper proposals, clearer business cases, more disciplined follow-up, and faster committee execution.

Use AI to strengthen execution, not replace judgment: draft faster, analyze better, and keep final decisions, relationship management, and community accountability with people.

Priority One
Foundation and Institutional Philanthropy
AI can help maintain a live grant calendar, summarize RFP requirements, draft first-pass letters of inquiry and proposal language, and convert SSHCO's core impact metrics into reusable narrative blocks tailored for different foundations.
Execution Multiplier
Priority Two
Corporate Sponsorship and Strategic Partnership Development
AI can support sponsor research, briefing preparation, follow-up drafting, and customized value propositions for corporate targets such as health systems, insurers, and regional employers.
Relationship Support
Priority Three
Board Development and Governance Readiness
AI can help map candidate profiles against SSHCO's target board model, identify capability gaps, summarize prospective members' backgrounds, and prepare outreach briefs for committee leaders.
Pipeline Support
Priority Four
Health System Community Benefit and MCO Value Proposition
AI-assisted analysis can organize service data, identify patterns in care coordination and transportation support, and help quantify the avoided-cost and access value SSHCO creates for hospitals and managed care organizations.
Business Case Development
🤖 Recommended Approach

SSHCO should begin with a small number of tightly defined AI pilots rather than a broad technology rollout. The first pilots should focus on grant and sponsorship drafting, board-development support, and MCO / community-benefit analytics.

Any AI use should follow three simple guardrails: keep human review on all external materials, avoid using AI as a decision-maker for community-facing or patient-sensitive matters, and document how data, assumptions, and outputs are being reviewed for accuracy, privacy, and equity.

This approach is consistent with current guidance from the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the Urban Institute's Medicaid AI research, and Stanford Social Innovation Review's guidance for health-focused nonprofits.

Section Six

Health System Community Benefit Partnerships

Every nonprofit hospital in our collaborative is legally required under Section 501(r) of the Internal Revenue Code to document and report community benefit spending. Many health systems struggle to demonstrate that their community benefit dollars are creating measurable, population-level impact. SSHCO is precisely the kind of documented, scalable, outcomes-oriented program that a health system's community benefit officer needs to be able to include in their IRS 990 Schedule H report.

UChicago Medicine invested $715 million in documented community benefits in fiscal year 2024. The relationship between UChicago Medicine and SSHCO is already established and productive — with patient advocates actively connecting residents to our services and 7 in 10 referred patients enrolling. What we need to do is formalize SSHCO as a documented, named line item in UChicago Medicine's community benefit portfolio, with a multi-year financial commitment that reflects the measurable value we deliver. This is not a grant request — it is a community benefit partnership formalization conversation, and it should be held with UChicago Medicine's community benefit and legal leadership, not their philanthropy team.

The same conversation needs to happen with each of the other 12 partner organizations in our collaborative. Each of them has their own community benefit obligations and their own interest in being able to point to SSHCO's outcomes as evidence that their community benefit spending is working. Formalizing SSHCO across multiple community benefit portfolios simultaneously reduces our dependence on any single partner and permanently embeds us in the South Side healthcare accountability ecosystem.

"Formalizing our role in partner hospitals' community benefit portfolios is not just a funding strategy. It is a recognition strategy — one that permanently positions SSHCO as an indispensable part of the South Side's health infrastructure in ways that are very difficult to defund."

🏥 Committee Action — Community Benefit Meetings

The committee should identify the community benefit officer and legal counsel at each of the 13 SSHCO partner organizations and request introductory meetings to discuss formalizing SSHCO's role in their community benefit portfolios. UChicago Medicine should be our first meeting given the depth of our existing relationship. We should go into each meeting with a one-page summary of the measurable outcomes we deliver on behalf of that institution's community benefit obligations specifically.

Section Seven

Earned Revenue & Organizational Capacity

The committee should also initiate a parallel conversation about earned revenue opportunities — not as a near-term fix for our funding gap, but as a 24–36 month development initiative that reduces our long-term dependence on any single source of philanthropic capital. SSHCO's community health worker training expertise, our shared technology platform, and our care coordination model represent intellectual capital that could be licensed or packaged for replication. Technical assistance fees, training and consulting revenue, and value-based payment arrangements with managed care organizations are all worthy of serious analysis.

The committee should also be candid with SSHCO's leadership about the staffing question. The scope of work outlined in this strategy — simultaneous engagement across four funding tracks, corporate partnership development, board recruitment, AI-enabled execution, and community benefit conversations across 13 institutions — is not realistically executable by a three-person volunteer committee alone alongside the operational demands of running the organization. We should make a recommendation to leadership about engaging a professional fundraising consultant or an interim development director with specific experience in health equity nonprofit fundraising. This investment will return multiples of its cost and will signal to major funders that SSHCO is institutionally serious about sustainability.
